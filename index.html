About Me

My name is Jacques Diec and I am a user experience (UX) and user interface (UI) designer residing in Los Angeles, California. I graduated from Cornell University in 2015. My greatest passion is interweaving art with technology; creativity with practicality. I also spend time outdoors hiking and cycling and have recently added a new hobby of going to the gym.

Stepvolution

Excercise with nostalgia

Overview

Stepvolution is an Android-based mobile application that aims to increase daily physical activity through walking amongst sedentary young adults. By using the phone’s accelerometer and GPS sensors, the application tracks the user’s daily step count and documents the locations in which they occur. In addition, the application provides persuasive (positive reinforcement) techniques via emotional feedback messages and images that serve as indicators of the user’s progress throughout the day. While there are a plethora of mobile-based applications (e.g. Moves) and wearable devices (e.g. pedometers) that collect real-time data on physical activity and location, very few of them address the psychological implications of exercise and thus fail to provide persuasive functionalities, which in the long-run, hinders progress by decreasing the user’s incentive and interest.

Background

Obesity is a common problem in developed countries, where food is abundant and easily accessible and there are many alternatives to transportation. Weight gain occurs when high caloric intake is followed by low caloric expenditure. Unchecked weight gain can lead to complications such as cardiovascular diseases, hypertension, and diabetes. The “Freshman 15” phenomenon is probably an exaggeration of the amount of weight gain that occurs amongst students who enter college, but there is nevertheless some truth behind it. Lack of physical activity is often reinforced by busyness, which is prevalent in the college community. Nevertheless, poor physical health affects mental and academic performance and we hope to mitigate that by encouraging minimal physical activity through walking.

The Role of Ubiquitous Mobile Technologies on Physical Health
Ubiquitous mobile technologies (e.g. text, internet browsing, social media, etc.) oftentimes decreases incentive for physical activity by offering distractions. Although they have improved many aspects of our lives, they are not replacements for the required daily physical activity we need to stay healthy. We hope to use sensory data provided by mobile devices in a way that is meaningful to people in regards to increasing their awareness of certain lifestyle choices and giving them the necessary incentive for improvement. Although our app does not collect data on other factors that affect one’s physical health, such as diet and mental wellbeing, we believe that giving people incentive to engage regularly in a simple activity as walking will inspire them to make other healthy life choices in the future. Given that many overweight people concern about their health, but are nevertheless too burdened to make significant changes to their current lifestyle choices, a ubiquitous application that is easily deplorable (almost everyone carries a mobile phone) and for the most part, non-intrusive, seems to be a very reasonable approach to addressing this issue on a day-to-day basis.

Relevant Studies & Literature

User Testing & Study

Selection of participants We sampled ten on-campus Cornell University undergraduates, aged 18-25 (inclusive). Of the ten participants, there were six males and three females. We informed our volunteers of the background and motivations for our study and answered any questions that they might have prior to obtaining their (verbal) consent. Pre-study phase We conducted an anonymous online and/or in-person survey (Figure 2) depending on the person’s availability, that collected information regarding each participant’s age, gender, college and major, and graduation year. Furthermore, the survey also collected information regarding their daily and weekly schedules, as well as their (estimated) time spent walking (daily and weekly) and their incentives (or lack of) for doing so. The mean response was 45 minutes daily to 120 minutes a week. The mean response for estimated step count was 8,000 steps a day (the actual mean statistics were rounded to the nearest whole number for baseline and familiarity measures). Main Study Phase We allocated half of our participants to the control group and the rest to the experimental group. Members of the control group were asked to install A Step at a Time (Asaat), a baseline application that does not include a persuasive gamification feature, onto their devices, while members of the experimental group were asked to install Stepvolution. Participants we asked to conduct their daily activities with their phones in their pockets. Both applications were left to run in the background, collecting their step count and location simultaneously. Participants were told that they could open the application to view their information, including the heat map. We collected data from our participants via screenshots at the end of each day. To avoid the risk of overloading their phones in memory, we asked that to uninstall their applications and reinstall them the following day (after they have reported to us their data).

World Life Expectancy

Description/caption

In this project, we were interested in measuring the effects of different variables on overall life expectancy in every country (and some sovereign territories) in the world. The overall life expectancy for each country and territory is our test variable. It is the mean of the overall life expectancy data for males and females from all age groups, demographics, and income levels. We introduced three control variables: Gross Domestic Product (GDP), ratio of national healthcare expenditure (public and private) to GDP, and total population.
